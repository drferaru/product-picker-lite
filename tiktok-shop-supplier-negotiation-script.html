Copyable script

MOQ and sample-price pushback

Send this when the supplier tries to move you straight into bulk quantity before the product has passed your sample and content-proof checks.

Hi [Supplier], thanks for the quote. Before we place a larger order, we need to run a small proof batch first. Please confirm: 1. Sample unit price and shipping cost to [country/region]. 2. Minimum sample quantity available for this SKU. 3. Lowest first-order quantity after the sample passes QC. 4. Photos of current packaging, variant labels, and included accessories. 5. Replacement policy if the sample arrives damaged, wrong variant, incomplete, or materially different from the listing. 6. Lead time for sample shipment and for the first small reorder. If the sample passes inspection and the first content test is clear, we can discuss a larger MOQ. We cannot commit to bulk quantity before those checks.

Pass signal: the supplier answers each line with numbers, photos, and terms. Reject signal: they keep repeating MOQ, best price, or “quality guaranteed” without proof.

Counteroffer

When they insist on bulk MOQ

Use a simple gate: sample pass, QC pass, content-proof pass, then first small reorder. Do not make the supplier's MOQ your validation plan.

I understand the standard MOQ is [quantity]. For this first test, we need to protect both sides by proving the product before bulk purchase. Here is the structure we can move forward with: Step 1: sample order at [quantity] units. Step 2: QC check against packaging, variant, defect, and listing match. Step 3: small first reorder at [quantity] units if the sample passes. Step 4: larger MOQ discussion only after the product survives real buyer/content testing. If that structure is not possible, we will test a different supplier for this SKU.

Watch signal: a supplier who refuses any proof batch may still be legitimate, but it is a poor fit for a first product test with limited cash.

Re-score gate

If the negotiation gets fuzzy, re-score the product before paying.

Supplier friction is part of product validation. If the economics only work after a risky MOQ, the product may be worse than it looked in the first idea pass.
